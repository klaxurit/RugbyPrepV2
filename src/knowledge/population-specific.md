# Population-Specific Adaptations — RugbyPrep

> Adjustments for specific player populations that deviate from the "default" profile
> (adult male, intermediate level, in-season).
> Sources: NSCA position stands, rugby-specific cohort studies, sports medicine consensus.

---

## 1. Female Players

### Physiological Differences Relevant to Training

**Hormonal cycle (menstrual phase)**
- **Follicular phase** (days 1–14, low estrogen at menstruation, rising afterward): typically higher pain tolerance, better adaptation to strength/power training. **Best time for high-intensity S&C.**
- **Ovulatory phase** (around day 14): peak estrogen → ligament laxity increases. **Higher ACL risk during pivoting/cutting.** Emphasize neuromuscular control.
- **Luteal phase** (days 15–28): progesterone rises, potential mood/fatigue impact. **Monitor recovery more closely.** May need load reduction.
- Relevant app implication: if cycle tracking is ever added, flag high-intensity sessions during ovulation phase.

**ACL Injury Risk**
- Female rugby players have 2–3× higher ACL injury risk vs. males (hormonal + anatomical factors)
- **Priority:** ACL prevention program should be embedded in all female player programs
- Exercises to emphasize: Nordic curl, single-leg Romanian deadlift, lateral band walks, deceleration drills
- Reference: Hewett et al. 2005, AJSM

**Strength Levels and Relative Loads**
- Absolute strength lower (expected), but relative strength (per kg BW) comparable when trained
- Lower initial 1RM estimates — use conservative baseline when no test data available
- Power development timeline: slightly slower initial adaptation but similar long-term ceiling

**Recovery**
- Similar recovery kinetics when matched for training load
- Iron needs higher (menstruation) — nutrition note for AI coach: flag fatigue that could indicate anemia

### Program Adjustments
- No structural program changes required (same periodization, same block types)
- **Increase prehab volume** (knee/hip stability): add one extra prehab slot where possible
- **CMJ/sprint baselines:** see `athletic-testing.md` — female baselines ~10–15% lower absolute values
- **Volume cap:** same MEV/MAV structure, slightly more conservative initial load progression

## 2. Under-18 Players (Youth Athletes)

### Growth and Development Considerations

**Ossification and Growth Plates**
- Growth plates (physes) remain open until age 16–18 in most athletes
- **Risk:** Traction apophysitis (Osgood-Schlatter at knee, Sever's at heel, Little Leaguer's at shoulder)
- **Contraindication:** Avoid heavy loads through growth plates (max strength work > 85% 1RM)
- **Safe:** Bodyweight, bands, moderate loads (≤ 75% 1RM), technique-focused work

**Neuromuscular Development**
- Pre-pubescent: neural adaptation is primary mechanism — strength gains without hypertrophy
- Post-pubescent (Tanner 3+): hormonal environment supports hypertrophy, similar to adults
- **Recommendation:** Focus on technique and movement quality until Tanner stage 3+

**Bone Stress**
- High training frequency + impact sport + growth → risk of stress reactions
- Monitor for persistent localized bone pain (shin, foot, lower back)
- **Weekly volume cap for U16:** 60–70% of adult volumes; **U18:** 80–90%

### Program Adjustments

| Parameter | Adult default | U18 adjustment | U16 adjustment |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|----------------|
| Max intensity | 85–90% 1RM | 75% 1RM max | 65% 1RM max |
| Weekly sets/MG (strength) | 14–24 | 10–16 | 8–12 |
| Deload frequency | Every 4 weeks | Every 3 weeks | Every 3 weeks |
| Plyometric volume | Normal | Reduce 30% | Reduce 50% |
| Olympic lifts | Permitted | Only with coach supervision | Not recommended |

**Priority exercises (U18):**
- Hinge patterns (RDL, hip thrust) before heavy squats
- Push-up mastery before bench press
- Row variations before weighted pull-ups
- Core stability (anti-extension, anti-rotation) emphasis

**Contact sports note:** For rugby U18, limit contact training volume during peak growth velocity (typically 12–14 boys, 10–12 girls).

## 3. Return After Long Break (> 6 Weeks)

### Detraining Timeline

After 6+ weeks of inactivity:
| Parameter | Loss rate | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------|
| Cardiovascular fitness | ~10% per week first 2 weeks, then slower | Most rapidly lost |
| Muscular strength | 5–10% over 8–12 weeks | Slower than cardio |
| Neuromuscular patterns | Variable — technique degrades | Regain fast with practice |
| Tendon/ligament conditioning | Very slow, 12+ weeks to rebuild | High injury risk on rapid return |

**Key insight:** Muscle strength returns relatively quickly (memory via satellite cells), but connective tissue (tendons, ligaments, cartilage) takes much longer. The most common post-break injury is **tendon overload**, not muscle tear.

### Return Protocol (Progressive Re-acclimatization)

#### Week 1–2: Tissue Preparation
- Volume: 40–50% of target volume
- Intensity: RPE 5–6 (very comfortable)
- Focus: Movement pattern restoration, tendon loading (eccentric emphasis)
- No HIIT, no plyometrics
- S&C: Starter-level recipes regardless of actual fitness level

#### Week 3–4: Progressive Loading
- Volume: 60–70% of target volume
- Intensity: RPE 6–7
- Begin light plyometric work (box step, low hurdles)
- Introduce match-specific movements at 50–60% intensity

#### Week 5–6: Reintegration
- Volume: 80–90% of target volume
- Intensity: RPE 7–8
- Full plyometric progression resumes
- Contact activities can resume if no musculoskeletal issues

#### Week 7+: Normal Program
- Return to standard periodization

### Red Flags After Long Break
- Any new joint pain > 3/10 during early return weeks → immediately reduce volume by 50%
- Tendon pain (patellar, Achilles, shoulder): use eccentric protocols, do NOT push through
- If break was due to injury: follow `return-to-play-criteria.md` protocol instead

### App Implementation Note
If a player indicates they've been inactive > 6 weeks (no training logs), the program engine should:
1. Override to starter-level recipes for weeks 1–2
2. Apply 50% volume modifier
3. Flag in AI coach response: suggest progressive return protocol

## 4. Older Athletes (35+)

*Note: Not currently a primary target demographic, but relevant for veteran/masters rugby.*

### Key Differences
- **Recovery time:** 48–72h between intense sessions (vs. 24–48h for < 35)
- **Connective tissue:** Slower tendon remodeling — prioritize eccentric loading and gradual progression
- **Hormonal decline:** Testosterone/growth hormone decrease → slower hypertrophy, more important to maintain strength with frequency
- **Heat regulation:** Less efficient sweating — hydration monitoring more important

### Adjustments
- Reduce intensity to RPE 7–8 max for strength work
- Increase rest periods between sets (3–4 min vs. 2 min)
- Deload every 3 weeks instead of 4
- Prioritize mobility and tissue quality work
